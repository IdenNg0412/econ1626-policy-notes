# Problem & Context
## Task automation 
The AI-driven task automation process operates by collecting and analyzing large volumes of data from diverse sources, which enables algorithms to identify relevant patterns and make decisions while continuously improving their performance through each iteration, all without the need for human intervention (Reddy and Roy 2025). AI tools can be integrated into existing business systems seamlessly to perform tasks with greater speed, consistency, and accuracy than human labor, particularly in repetitive and codifiable areas (Filippi et al. 2023). Economically, these AI tools allow businesses to save money by decreasing reliance on human labor while simultaneously boosting productivity, as they can perform activities continuously without needing breaks like humans, leading to changes in how human values are conceived in the labor market (Filippi et al. 2023). This results in a reduction of labor demand in predictable and standardized occupations, such as administrative and clerical roles, which contributes to short-term unemployment and labor market adjustments that place the greatest pressure on middle-skill workers whose tasks are most vulnerable to substitution (Stephany 2026).
## Task Augmentation
The integration of AI across industries is not limited to task automation but also involves task augmentation, where AI functions as a complementary tool that enhances human productivity and decision-making rather than fully replacing workers (Grimaldo 2025). Through collaborative AI-human partnership, AI performs data-intensive and repetitive tasks while providing analytical insights and recommendations, enabling workers to focus on strategic and complex tasks that require creativity, judgment, and interpersonal skills (Lardi 2025). However, the benefits of AI augmentation are distributed unevenly across the workforce, as higher-skilled workers with advanced digital capabilities combined with specialized skills and training in technical domains are better positioned to leverage AI tools effectively, resulting in greater productivity gains and wage increases compared to lower-skilled workers (Uppili 2026). Consequently, AI-driven augmentation may intensify existing skill and wage inequalities, as those without access to advanced training and digital resources may struggle to compete in AI-driven labour market (Alam 2026).
## Task Creation
As AI adoption expands across industries, demand is increasing not only for AI-related occupations such as AI engineers, data scientists, and AI ethics consultants, but also in infrastructure-related roles driven by growing investment in data centers associated with AI development (Goldman Sachs 2026a). Furthermore, AI innovation may generate entirely new business models and specialized occupations that previously did not exist, contributing to broader employment opportunities across emerging industries (Goldman Sachs 2026b). However, these new labor demands often require specialized skills in technical domains such as AI ethics, human-AI collaboration, and digital literacy that are not widely sufficient, creating barriers for displaced workers transitioning from declining occupations and those with less access to education and technology (Alam 2026). As a result, although AI creates new employment opportunities, rapidly shifting skill demands could intensify skills shortages and transitional inequality in the labour market (Bonney et al. 2024).
# Analysis of Impacts 
AI represents a hybrid form of innovation, which can function incrementally as an optimization tool that amplifies existing capability to enhance efficiency while also serving as a disruptive force that reshapes the nature of work thanks to its generative capabilities (Randieri 2025).  However, it is noticeable that the AI adoption wave is reshaping the labor market through uneven distribution across workers and occupations, leading to significant changes in employment patterns, wage structures, and industrial dynamics, particularly affecting low-skilled jobs while creating new opportunities.

AI-driven automation reduces demand for structured and repetitive occupations such as administrative support, clerical work, and customer services, which are particularly vulnerable because AI systems can perform these tasks faster and at lower cost with computational performance compared to human labour (Azpúrua 2026). At the same time, while AI also creates employment opportunities in technology-related sectors such as software engineering, cybersecurity, and AI governance, these occupations are concentrated among highly skilled workers, meaning that these employment gains may not be fully guaranteed to compensate for losses in lower and middle-skilled workers (Gimbel et al. 2025). Furthermore, rapid shifts in labour market demands often require significant time for workforce adaptation due to skill mismatches and transitional barriers (Wishart-Smith 2024). Therefore, displaced workers from declining occupations may struggle to adapt and transition to emerging roles that require higher levels of digital and analytical capability, contributing to short-term employment, underemployment, and economic anxiety during transition periods (World Economic Forum 2026). These deep-seated anxieties are reflected not only through dystopian popular culture such as “The Terminator” and “Blade Runner”, but also through economic phenomena like the Jevons paradox, where efficiency gains and lower production costs prompt wider adoption of AI-driven labour substitution across industries (Goldman Sachs 2026b; Wishart-Smith 2024).

## AI as the main driver of inequality, wage, and industry

Within nations, AI may deepen labour market polarization across income, geographic, and educational groups as productivity gains and wage growth are increasingly concentrated among urban, highly educated, and higher-income workers who possess the skills and infrastructure necessary to effectively utilize AI tools, particularly in occupations augmented by AI (OECD 2023). In contrast, workers in lower-paid routine workers exposed to AI automation are more likely to experience lower wage gains or job displacement due to the highly automatable nature of their tasks and changing perception of human labour value within increasingly automated production systems (Alam 2026). Consequently, these trends may contribute to a declining labour share of income, weakened labour bargaining power, and broader structural inequality across regions, demographic groups and firms, particularly affecting workers with lower educational backgrounds or limited access to digital training opportunities (Alam 2026). <br>
Additionally, AI may increase market concentration within industries if its development and deployment are dominated by a few large firms such as Amazon, Meta, Google, and Microsoft, which has established themselves as essential suppliers within the ecosystem through substantial advantages in capital and computing infrastructure, and network effects (Corrigan 2025). Consequently, smaller and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) may struggle to adopt AI technologies at a comparable scale, thereby reducing competition, reinforcing barriers to market entry and hindering innovation (Hagiu and Wright 2025). Although AI-driven task recomposition can increase labour productivity and reduce operational costs, the unequal distribution of AI capabilities and economic gains may generate structural imbalances that are unsustainable for long-term economic growth (Wang 2025).

From a geopolitical perspective, the impact of AI on the labour market varies across countries depending on their capacity to innovate, adopt, and the societal acceptance of automation, creating new forms of inequality (Alam 2026). Advanced and emerging economies such as the United States and China are better positioned to capture AI-driven productivity gains, high-skilled employment opportunities, and economic growth due to substantial investments in AI development, digital infrastructure, and workforce readiness (Alam 2026). In contrast, many developing economies, particularly in Latin America, continue to face structural barriers, including limited digital infrastructure, inadequate workforce skills, and weaker social protection systems, which lead to widening inequality and economic divergence between countries (Parliament of Australia 2025).

## Option 1: Reskilling and Lifelong Learning Plan 
As AI reshapes skill demands across industries, the first policy option is to expand government-funded reskilling and lifelong learning programs to help workers transition into AI-complementary occupations (Ersanlı et al. 2025). This requires reevaluating existing education frameworks through curriculum and pedagogical reforms in subsidized digital training, micro-credentials, vocational education, and partnerships between industries, universities, and TAFE institutions (Tran, Vo and Le 2024). The policy also promotes interdisciplinary AI education integrated not only into computer science but also across fields such as healthcare, business, and social sciences to strengthen digital literacy, adaptability, and analytical capabilities across the workforce (Baragas et al. 2024). <br>
The rationale for this approach is that improving human capital through reskilling and education programs can help workers adapt to changing labour demands, reduce structural unemployment, and support long-term productivity growth (Ersanlı et al. 2025). However, participation barriers such as time constraints, financial limitations, and unequal institutional resources may reduce policy effectiveness, particularly for disadvantaged workers and underfunded educational institutions (Tran, Vo and Le 2024).
## Option 2: Wage Insurance and Labour Adjustment Support
The second policy approach serves as a short-term countermeasure involving wage insurance and labor adjustment assistance for workers displaced by AI automation. This policy would establish government-funded agencies that offer job search assistance and career counseling services to provide necessary consultation for the relocation of displaced workers and to ease the social anxiety connected with job loss (DEWR 2025). These employment services aim to provide place-based employment support through local jobs programs that connect displaced workers with local employers and new job opportunities within their communities (DEWR 2025). <br>
In addition, the policy introduces specialized wage insurance schemes that provide temporary financial relief by covering the percentage of wage differences and reskilling funding for affected workers that provide financial stability while building new skills during the transition periods (Cuofano 2026). Eligibility and compensation could be assessed using an AI-Displacement Risk Index (AIDR) based on labor market data, corporate reporting, and occupational exposure to automation, enabling faster and more transparent claims processing to provide timely financial support during transitions caused by AI-driven layoffs (Erne 2025).
Although this policy is relatively feasible because it can build upon existing welfare and employment service systems, wage insurance and career transition services are only able to mitigate short-term displacement costs and do not directly address long-term skill mismatches or improve workforce adaptability to future technological change (Brioscú et al. 2024).
