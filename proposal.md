Policy proposal for AI's impact on labour market

# Executive Summary 
Artificial intelligence (AI) is reshaping labour markets through task automation, augmentation, and task creation, creating productivity gains and greater efficiency while disrupting workforce employment patterns and skill demands. Although AI can improve efficiency and create new employment opportunities, unequal access to and distribution of AI capabilities may intensify labour market polarisation, structural inequality, and workforce instability, particularly for routine and lower-skilled workers vulnerable to automation displacement.

This policy proposal evaluates three policy responses: reskilling and lifelong learning programs, wage insurance and labour adjustment support, and ethical AI workplace regulation. It recommends a combined policy package integrating workforce development, social protection, and regulatory oversight to address both the short and long-term impacts of AI on labour markets. Coordinated implementation between government, industry, and educational institutions is necessary to ensure effective policy implementation and support inclusive economic growth, workforce adaptability, and labour protections during technological transition.
# Problem & Context
## Task automation

The AI-driven task automation process operates by collecting and analyzing large volumes of data from diverse sources, which enables algorithms to identify relevant patterns and make decisions while continuously improving their performance through each iteration, all without the need for human intervention (Reddy and Roy 2025). Economically, AI-driven task automation enables firms to perform repetitive and codifiable tasks more efficiently and at lower cost than human labour, particularly in occupations involving administrative support, clerical work, and customer services (Filippi et al. 2023). Consequently, AI technologies are increasingly substituting routine and predictable tasks previously performed by human workers, contributing to a declining economic value of routine labour within increasingly automated production systems (Stephany 2026).

## Task Augmentation
<img width="1681" height="832" alt="Image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/9415a9c1-6a2b-405f-a5de-10fc6a596eac" />
Figure 2: Occupations most exposed to the AI augmentation effect. Source: Goldman Sachs 2026b <br>

The integration of AI across industries is not limited to task automation but also involves task augmentation, where AI functions as a complementary tool that enhances human productivity and decision-making rather than fully replacing workers (Grimaldo 2025). Through collaborative AI-human partnership, AI performs data-intensive and repetitive tasks while providing analytical insights and recommendations, enabling workers to focus on strategic and complex tasks that require creativity, judgment, and interpersonal skills (Lardi 2025). However, the benefits of AI augmentation are distributed unevenly across the workforce, as higher-skilled workers with advanced digital capabilities combined with specialized skills and training in technical domains are better positioned to leverage AI tools effectively, resulting in greater productivity gains and wage increases compared to lower-skilled workers (Uppili 2026). Consequently, AI-driven augmentation may intensify existing skill and wage inequalities, as those without access to advanced training and digital resources may struggle to compete in AI-driven labour market (Alam 2026).

As AI adoption expands across industries, demand is increasing not only for AI-related occupations such as AI engineers, data scientists, and AI ethics consultants, but also in infrastructure-related roles driven by growing investment in data centers associated with AI development (Goldman Sachs 2026a). Furthermore, AI innovation may generate entirely new business models and specialized occupations that previously did not exist, contributing to broader employment opportunities across emerging industries (Goldman Sachs 2026b). However, these new labor demands often require specialized skills in technical domains such as AI ethics, human-AI collaboration, and digital literacy that are not widely sufficient, creating barriers for displaced workers transitioning from declining occupations and those with less access to education and technology (Alam 2026). As a result, although AI creates new employment opportunities, rapidly shifting skill demands could intensify skills shortages and transitional inequality in the labour market (Bonney et al. 2024).

# Analysis of Impacts 

AI represents a hybrid form of innovation, which can function incrementally as an optimization tool that amplifies existing capability to enhance efficiency while also serving as a disruptive force that reshapes the nature of work thanks to its generative capabilities (Randieri 2025).  However,  the AI adoption is increasingly reshaping the labor market through uneven distribution across workers and occupations, leading to significant changes in employment patterns, wage structures, and industrial dynamics, particularly affecting low-skilled jobs while creating new opportunities.

## Employment displacement and transformation 
<img width="1368" height="1072" alt="Image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/11aa541c-778b-43bd-b994-8bf662bc1dbc" />
Figure 4: Share of occupation employment exposed to automation by AI in the US. Source: Goldman Sachs 2026a <br>

AI-driven automation reduces demand for structured and routine occupations such as administrative support, clerical work, and customer services, as AI systems can perform these reptitive tasks more efficiently and at lower cost with computational performance compared to human labour (Azpúrua 2026). Although AI also creates new employment opportunities areas such as software engineering, cybersecurity, and AI governance, these occupations are concentrated among highly skilled workers, meaning that these employment gains may not be fully guaranteed to compensate for losses in lower and middle-skilled workers (Gimbel et al. 2025). Furthermore, rapid shifts in labour market demands often require significant time for workforce adaptation due to skill mismatches and transitional barriers (Wishart-Smith 2024). Therefore, displaced workers from declining occupations may struggle to adapt and transition to emerging roles that require higher levels of digital and analytical capability, contributing to short-term unemployment and economic anxiety during transition periods (Stephany 2026). These deep-seated anxieties are reflected through economic phenomena like the Jevons paradox, where efficiency gains and lower production costs prompt wider adoption of AI-driven labour substitution across industries (Goldman Sachs 2026b; Wishart-Smith 2024).

## AI as the main driver of inequality, wage, and industry
<img width="1396" height="1008" alt="Image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/6ddd03f2-ee12-424a-a3b2-c97dcd18fef8" />
Figure 5: Payroll employment by industry exposure to AI. Source: Goldman Sachs 2026b <br>

Within nations, AI may deepen labour market polarization as productivity gains and wage growth are increasingly concentrated among urban, highly educated, and higher-income workers who possess the skills and infrastructure necessary to effectively utilise AI tools (OECD 2023). In contrast, workers in lower-paid routine occupations exposed to AI automation are more likely to experience wage stagnation or job displacement due to the highly automatable nature of their tasks and the declining economic value of routine labour within increasingly automated production systems (Alam 2026). Consequently, these trends may contribute to a declining labour share of income, weakened labour bargaining power, and broader structural inequality across regions and demographic groups, particularly affecting workers with lower educational backgrounds or limited access to digital training opportunities (Alam 2026).

Additionally, AI may increase market concentration within industries if its development and deployment are dominated by a few large firms such as Amazon, Meta, Google, and Microsoft, which have established themselves as essential suppliers within the AI ecosystem through substantial advantages in capital, computing infrastructure, and network effects (Corrigan 2025). Consequently, smaller and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) may struggle to adopt AI technologies at a comparable scale, reducing competition and reinforcing barriers to market entry (Hagiu and Wright 2025). Although AI-driven task recomposition can increase labour productivity and reduce operational costs, the unequal distribution of AI capabilities and economic gains may generate structural imbalances that are unsustainable for long-term economic growth (Wang 2025).

From a geopolitical perspective, the labour-market impacts of AI also vary globally depending on countries’ capacity to innovate, adopt AI technologies, and socially accept automation, creating new forms of inequality (Alam 2026). Advanced and emerging economies such as the United States and China are better positioned to capture AI-driven productivity gains, high-skilled employment opportunities, and economic growth due to substantial investments in AI development, digital infrastructure, and workforce readiness (Alam 2026). In contrast, many developing economies, particularly in Latin America, continue to face structural barriers including limited digital infrastructure, inadequate workforce skills, and weaker social protection systems, increasing the risk of widening global inequality (Parliament of Australia 2025).

## Option 1: Reskilling and Lifelong Learning Plan 
As AI reshapes skill demands across industries, the first policy option is to expand government-funded reskilling and lifelong learning programs to help workers transition into AI-complementary occupations (Ersanlı et al. 2025). This requires reevaluating existing education frameworks through curriculum and pedagogical reforms in subsidized digital training, micro-credentials, vocational education, and partnerships between industries, universities, and TAFE institutions (Tran, Vo and Le 2024). The policy also promotes interdisciplinary AI education integrated not only into computer science but also across fields such as healthcare, business, and social sciences to strengthen digital literacy, adaptability, and analytical capabilities across the workforce (Baragas et al. 2024). <br>

The rationale for this approach is that improving human capital through reskilling and education programs can help workers adapt to changing labour demands, reduce structural unemployment, and support long-term productivity growth (Ersanlı et al. 2025). However, participation barriers such as time constraints, financial limitations, and unequal institutional resources may reduce policy effectiveness, particularly for disadvantaged workers and underfunded educational institutions (Tran, Vo and Le 2024).

## Option 2: Wage Insurance and Labour Adjustment Support
The second policy approach serves as a short-term countermeasure involving wage insurance and labor adjustment assistance for workers displaced by AI automation. This policy would establish government-funded agencies that offer job search assistance and career counseling services to provide necessary consultation for the relocation of displaced workers and to ease the social anxiety connected with job loss (DEWR 2025). These employment services aim to provide place-based employment support through local jobs programs that connect displaced workers with local employers and new job opportunities within their communities (DEWR 2025). <br>
In addition, the policy introduces specialized wage insurance schemes that provide temporary financial relief by covering a proportion of wage losses and reskilling funding for affected workers that provide financial stability while building new skills during the transition periods (Cuofano 2026). Eligibility and compensation could be assessed using an AI-Displacement Risk Index (AIDR) based on labor market data, corporate reporting, and occupational exposure to automation, enabling faster and more transparent claims processing to provide timely financial support during transitions caused by AI-driven layoffs (Erne 2025).
Although this policy is relatively feasible because it can build upon existing welfare and employment service systems, wage insurance and career transition services are only able to mitigate short-term displacement costs and do not directly address long-term skill mismatches or improve workforce adaptability to future technological change (Brioscú et al. 2024).

## Option 3: Ethical AI Regulation in Workplaces
The third policy option is the introduction of ethical AI regulation in the workplace to strengthen labour rights and protect workers from harmful AI-driven employment practices. This approach prioritises worker wellbeing by requiring companies and public agencies to ensure that AI systems are implemented responsibly rather than solely for short-term productivity gains (AFL-CIO 2025). The policy strengthens labour rights and expands opportunities for collective bargaining by mandating employer consultation with workers and labour representatives for the adoption of new workplace technologies (Gutsu and Spitsyna 2024). Furthermore, it also requires advance notice of workforce reductions accompanied by meaningful reemployment support, income assistance, and retraining initiatives for workers affected by AI-related displacement (Gutsu et al. 2023). Finally, the policy strengthens protections against discriminatory algorithmic practices and excessive workplace surveillance by requiring transparent and explainable AI decision-making processes, active human oversight, and stronger safeguards for workers’ civil rights and collective bargaining rights (Majumdar and Misra 2026). Although the rationale of this policy is to protect workers from harmful AI-driven practices, stricter regulation could increase compliance costs and reduce flexibility and slow technological innovation for businesses, particularly smaller firms (Kelley and Rogers 2025). 

# Recommended Package & Implementation Plan
Overall, each policy option addresses different dimensions of AI-driven labour market disruption through complementary strengths and policy objectives. Therefore, a combined policy package integrating reskilling and lifelong learning programs, wage insurance and labour adjustment assistance, and ethical AI workplace regulation is recommended to address both the short and long-term impacts of AI on labour markets. Reskilling and lifelong learning programs strengthen long-term workforce adaptability and productivity growth by preparing both current workers and future generations with necessary skills for AI-complementary occupations (Ersanlı et al. 2025). Meanwhile, wage insurance and labour adjustment assistance provide immediate economic and social support for workers displaced during labour-market transitions (Erne 2025). In contrast, ethical AI regulation strengthens labour rights, transparency, and accountability to protect workers from harmful and unsustainable AI-driven employment practices (Gutsu and Spitsyna 2024). Together, these policies provide a balanced approach that combines workforce development, social protection, and regulatory oversight to ensure that AI adoption supports economic progress while maintaining human value and labour protections within society.

By proactively supporting workers through technological transition, the government can promote more inclusive economic growth while reducing the risk of long-term structural inequality and social dislocation caused by uneven AI adoption.
# Risk and Mitigations
| Risk                                     | Potential Impact                                                          | Mitigation Strategy                                                                |
| ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Low participation in reskilling programs | Limits workforce adaptability and policy effectiveness                    | Provide subsidies, flexible online learning, and employer-supported training       |
| Persistent skills mismatch               | Workers may remain unemployed despite retraining                          | Align training programs with industry demand through regular labour market reviews |
| Regulatory burden on firms               | Ethical AI regulations may slow AI adoption and increase compliance costs | Introduce phased implementation and simplified compliance support for SMEs         |
| Fiscal pressure on government            | Long-term funding requirements may strain public budgets                  | Conduct periodic policy evaluations and prioritise targeted funding allocation     |

While the proposed policy package may improve labour market resilience and equity, several implementation risks may affect policy effectiveness that requires a coordinated and adaptive implementation strategy to minimise these risks. Regular policy evaluation and collaboration between government, industry, and educational institutions is essential to improve policy responsiveness and ensure AI-driven economic benefits are distributed more equitably across the labour market.
